Diwali Can Be Anywhere

“Diwali tomorrow,” sang Deepak.

“Yes,” said Dad. “But no diyas and firecrackers permitted here.”

They’d newly moved from India to California.

 

Nita hugged her brother. “You know why, Deeps? The homes are wooden. Flammable.”

“Sparklers, rockets, noisemakers,” Deepak wailed. “I want them, Nita.”

Nita stepped out. 

 

“Wish I could make Deepak a Diwali celebration.”

Dry leaves popped underfoot.

Wind-swirled foliage made rangoli patterns.

 

“Sound and color. This is Diwali. Think, Nita.” 

“No firecrackers. No diyas. What can I use instead?”

Nita and Mom went shopping for:

Christmas lights,

Bubble wrap,

Popcorn,

Light-up balls,

Balloons.

On Diwali night the Christmas lights shone bright “Look, Papa, lights without diyas.”

Deepak unrolled the bubble wrap. Ziggety-zag he jumped. Pip-pop! Noisemakers!!

They bounced the balls. Boing! Boing!

“Sparklers,” clapped Deepak.

Wall-to-wall strings carried little boxes with balloons attached.

 

Nita blew into a balloon and let go. The box zoomed across like a rocket. Whoosh!

The popcorn popped.

They feasted on flaky samosas, crispy kachoris, mounds of pulao, pooris, curries, kebabs, honey-sweet gulabjamuns, saffron-infused kheer.

Papa beamed. “The Festival of Lights.”

“Thank you Nita.” A huge Deepak hug. “Best Diwali ever.”

Nita smiled. “Diwali can be anywhere.”
